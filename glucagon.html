glucagon

GlucaGen

Glucagon is a peptide hormone produced naturally by the pancreas, and opposes insulin by increasing the levels of glucose in the bloodstream through glycogenolysis.

In the prehospital environment, Glucagon is used as a second-line treatment for hypoglycemia, or as a first-line treatment when IV access is unavailable or difficult. Since it relies on metabolic stores of glycogen, Glucagon may not be effective in patients without adequate glycogen reserves.

Glucagon is administered via the intramuscular (IM) route and is available in both standard vials and autoinjectors.

Mechanism of Action

The pancreas releases glucagon naturally when the blood glucose level falls below a certain point. Glucagon travels to the liver and stimulates it to convert stored glycogen into glucose, thus increasing the blood glucose level.

There is anecdotal evidence that glucagon is beneficial in β- and calcium-channel blocker overdose. The suspected mechanism of action is twofold: first, there is some evidence that it works as a β agonist, increasing inotropic and chronotropic effects within the myocardium.

Glucagon also causes relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter, which is occasionally useful in the (in-hospital) treatment of food stuck in the esophagus.

Glycogenesis and Glycogenolysis

Understanding how the body processes glucose is necessary to understand how glucagon and dextrose work.

Glycogenisis is the process of converting unused glucose into glycogen and storing it the liver and muscles for later use.

Glycogenolysis is the process of breaking down stored glycogen back into glucose when the body needs extra energy.

Glycolysis is the metabolic pathway responsible for breaking down glucose into usable energy. First, glucose is broken down into two ATP molecules, two molecules of a coenzyme (NAD), and two molecules of pyruvate. The pyruvate is later processed during the Krebs cycle, and the overall process yields a total of 36 molecules of ATP for each molecule of glucose.

Gluconeogenesis is the opposite of glycolysis, and occurs when the body is starved of carbohydrates to convert. Gluconeogenesis uses many of the same chemical processes as glycolysis, but in reverse. Gluconeogenesis produces glucose by converting pyruvate into glucose.

Indications

  • Acute management of hypoglycemia (glucometer ≤ 60 mg/dL)
  • Acute management of severe hypoglycemia when administration of glucose is not feasible.
  • Facilitate radiographic examination of the GI tract.
  • Calcium-channel blocker and β-blocker agent overdose.

Contraindications

Contraindicated Use Caution
  • Hypersensitivity to beef or pork proteins, glycerin or phenol.
  • History of:
    • insulinoma, a known pancreatic tumor
    • phenochromocytoma, a known adrenal gland tumor

Glucagon relies on the body having adequate glycogen stores. In cases where the body has been in either a prolonged hypermetabolic state, or has a carbohydrate-deficient intake, glucagon may not be effective.

Routes of Administration

Acute hypoglycemia

1mg IM/IN.

Ca-Channel Blocker/β-blocker overdose

3-10mg IV/IO.

Anaphylaxis refractory to epinephrine

1-4mg slow IV push.

Glucagon needs reconstituted by adding the supplied sterile water with the glucagon powder.

In Pennsylvania, AEMTs are not permitted to administer glucagon via the IV route.
