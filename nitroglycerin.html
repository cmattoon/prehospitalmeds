nitroglycerin

Nitrostat, NitroLingual, Nitro-Bid

Nitroglycerin was popularized by Alfred Nobel, who used it to manufacture dynamite. Shortly thereafter, factory workers began noticing strange symptoms whose timing seemed suspicious. Some workers experienced headaches during the work week that resolved over the weekend, while other workers (with a history of angina pectoris) noticed that their symptoms resolved during the week, only to return on the weekend.

This led to the investigation of nitroglycerin as an anti-anginal medication, and it's still in wide use today as the first-line treatment for angina pectoris.

Mechanism of Action

Nitroglycerin's primary mechanism of action is to cause vasodilation. This creates a decrease in systemic vascular resistance (afterload) and hopefully increases blood flow and oxygenation through obstructed or constricted coronary arteries.

Although nitroglycerin has been in use for nearly 150 years, its exact mechanism of action wasn't fully explained until 1998. Physicians and physiologists thought it obvious that nitroglycerin caused vasodilation, but could not determine the specific pathway until 1998. Increasing evidence had shown nitric oxide (NO) to be released by vascular smooth muscle at a more rapid rate when in the presence of nitroglycerin.

In fact, the Nobel Prize for Medicine and Physiology that year was awarded to Furchgott, Ignarro and Murad for their discovery of Nitric Oxide's role in mediating vasodilation. They published their results in a paper titled "Nitric Oxide as a signaling molecule in the vascular system: an overview".

Indications

  • Acute management of hypoglycemia (glucometer ≤ 60 mg/dL)
  • Acute management of severe hypoglycemia when administration of glucose is not feasible.
  • Facilitate radiographic examination of the GI tract.
  • Calcium-channel blocker and β-blocker agent overdose.

Contraindications

Contraindicated Use Caution
  • Hypersensitivity to beef or pork proteins, glycerin or phenol.
  • History of:
    • insulinoma, a known pancreatic tumor
    • phenochromocytoma, a known adrenal gland tumor

Glucagon relies on the body having adequate glycogen stores. In cases where the body has been in either a prolonged hypermetabolic state, or has a carbohydrate-deficient intake, glucagon may not be effective.

Routes of Administration

Acute hypoglycemia

1mg IM/IN.

Ca-Channel Blocker/β-blocker overdose

3-10mg IV/IO.

Anaphylaxis refractory to epinephrine

1-4mg slow IV push.

Glucagon needs reconstituted by adding the supplied sterile water with the glucagon powder.

In Pennsylvania, AEMTs are not permitted to administer glucagon via the IV route.

Monitoring

Airway/Breathing
Severely hypoxic patients may take longer to respond to naloxone than usual. Since this is impossible to determine beforehand, it is important to support ventilation with a BVM attached to high-flow oxygen.

Proactive airway management is necessary due to the risk of vomiting post-administration. Since it's possible that the patient has taken other drugs that will not be affected by naloxone, it can't be assumed that the patient will be able to adequately protect their own airway.

While these patients rarely require intubation, it is not uncommon to place supraglottic/extraglottic airways (e.g., King LTD, Combitube) is sometimes required.
Cardiovascular
Opiates generally cause vasodilation and a decrease in blood pressure, causing a compensetory increase in heart rate. The reversal of these effects can compromise hemodynamic stability. Common side-effects include hypertension, hypotension, dizziness, and tachycardia.
